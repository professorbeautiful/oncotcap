The BAX apoptosis model

This elementary model demonstrates a few capabilities of OncoTCap 4.

On the next screen is a schematic of the key Statement Bundle, how it is based on a Statement Template, and how the parameter editor assists with specifying the functional state of the apoptosis mechanism.

On the following screen (the "editor"), the model is built by choosing statement bundles on the left and if desired editing the parameter values by clicking on the parameter names in the central column.

Of interest is that the activity of the mechanism depends on a functional p53 product, which is represented as a Characteristic, "p53_functionality". Note that p53_functionality is NOT an identifying Characteristic of the particular cell type, and could change as temporary conditions change, such as the presence of treatments, orsignals from neighboring cells. Many additional statements could be added to account for a variety of causes for the disabling of p53 function. In this case, p53_functionality depends only on the presence of at least one WT (wild-type) allele.

Two types of mutations of p53 are implemented. The first is an allele-level mutation, expressed in the statement:

        "For gene  p53 , the WT allele can mutate to a MUT allele with mutation rate 1e-4."

represents a packaging of the pair of model assumptions, that WT-WT -> WT-MUT can occur, and that WT-MUT to MUT-MUT can occur, and that WT -> MUT can occur. The first mutation rate is twice the second, since there are two target alleles. This is powered by two supporting statement bundles; to see how, go back to the Browser and trace the connections back through SB's, ST's, and CB's.

The second mutation is loss of heterozygosity, and is expressed in the statement:

Again, the transition WT-WT to WT happens with twice the frequency of WT-MUT to WT.

While these statements can applied to any gene, they cannot in one step be applied to all genes in the model. OncoTCap does not currently have an ontology-based rule engine. This is a problem that will be solved with integration with the caBIG "Enterprise Vocabulary Services".

It is interesting that the two mutation types can exist independently, and can also be combined. This is because two different Level Lists for a single Characteristic (in this case, the gene) are combined by taking the set union. ∠ ♠
